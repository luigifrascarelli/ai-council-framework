Section 05 · Open Question
AI Tools Administration
Who owns licensing, provisioning, access management, and tool configuration across orgs? This is unresolved and worth alignment.
▾
Why this matters
AI tool administration — who provisions users, manages licenses, configures permissions, monitors usage, and owns the renewal relationship — often falls through the cracks between IT, Procurement, and the business teams. Without a defined owner, you get shadow IT, cost overruns, security gaps, and inconsistent access. The questions below are designed to facilitate the right conversation before the Council launches.
Key questions to resolve
Ownership
Who is accountable for day-to-day tool administration — IT, a dedicated Admin role, or each org team?
If it's IT, do they have the context to manage AI-specific tools (token limits, model access tiers, prompt logging)? If it's the org, do they have the capacity?
Provisioning
How does a new SE, FDE, or CS team member get access to approved AI tools from day one?
Is access tied to role/profile in Okta or AD? Is there a manual request process? What's the SLA? Who approves and who fulfills?
License Management
Who tracks seat utilization, identifies waste, and manages renewals across all approved tools?
Today, multiple tools may be auto-renewing without anyone auditing active vs. inactive seats. This is a cost governance gap that Procurement and Finance need to close.
Configuration & Policy
Who owns the security configuration inside each tool — data retention policies, SSO, DLP integrations, audit logging?
Security needs to set the policy. But someone has to implement it inside each tool. Is that IT, Security, or the business team that owns the tool?
Usage Monitoring
Who reviews token consumption, flags anomalies, and surfaces low-impact usage patterns across teams?
The leaderboard handles SE visibility. But what about cross-org monitoring? Who sees the full picture and acts on it?
Tool Offboarding
When a tool is rejected or sunset, who ensures access is revoked, data is purged, and the contract is closed?
This is a security and legal exposure if not handled systematically. It needs to be defined in the governance framework before a tool ever goes live.
Three models to consider
Option A
Centralized IT Admin
IT owns all provisioning, configuration, and license management. Council defines policy; IT executes.
+ Consistent. Secure. Audit-ready. Clear SLAs.
− IT may lack AI context. Slower provisioning. Bottleneck risk.
Option B · Recommended
Dedicated AI Admin Role
A dedicated AI Tools Admin sits within the Council. Owns provisioning, config, monitoring, and renewals across all orgs.
+ Deep AI context. One throat to choke. Fast cycle times.
− New headcount or backfill required. Potential single point of failure.
Option C
Federated Org Admins
Each org (SE, FDE, PS, CS) has a designated Admin who manages their tools. Council coordinates policy only.
+ Org-specific context. Faster local execution.
− Inconsistent standards. Fragmented visibility. Higher overhead.
Decision criteria for the Council to align on
Volume of toolsHow many active tools are in the portfolio today vs. projected in 12 months? Under 5 tools may not justify a dedicated Admin.
Security postureAre any tools processing Tier 1 or Tier 2 data? If yes, IT or a dedicated Admin is the only viable option — federated is too risky.
Org autonomy needsIf SE, FDE, and CS need to move at different speeds with different tools, federated admins with shared policy rails may be the pragmatic call.
Budget realityA dedicated Admin role is the cleanest solution but requires headcount. If that's not available, Option A or C is the starting point with a roadmap to Option B.
